## Client Pitch {#client-pitch}

This project is focused on designing a mechanical solution to remove and neutralize Spotted Lanternfly (SLF) egg masses before they hatch and lead to vineyard infestations. SLF egg masses strongly adhere to rough, and often hard to reach surfaces and contain 30–60 eggs each, making them difficult to destroy using inconsistent manual methods. With no natural predators targeting egg masses and a 6–9 month incubation window, early-stage mechanical intervention presents a high-impact opportunity to reduce population growth.

We came up with the prototype concept of an integrated handheld scraper with alcohol delivery to improve removal effectiveness and reduce labor intensity. The goal is to create a cost-effective, field-ready tool that supports sustainable pest management and protects the $13B U.S. grape industry from fruit contamination and economic losses.

## Functional Prototype {#functional-prototype}

**Purpose:** The functional prototype is a high-reach, pole-mounted mechanical scraper designed to replace manual methods of removing Spotted Lanternfly egg masses. Building on a previous prototype that validated the handle and rod assembly, this iteration focused solely on testing the custom 3D-printed scraper head. The scraper head press-fits onto a wooden rod and is shaped to dislodge and collect egg masses from walls and outdoor surfaces at heights up to 10 feet.

**What Was Tested:**
- **Scraping efficacy:** 10 clay "egg masses" were placed at varying heights (29–200 cm) on a wall and scraped from multiple angles to simulate real field conditions.
- **Scraper size:** A visual audit during efficacy trials assessed whether the bowl geometry was large enough to capture detached material.
- **Blade durability:** An accelerated wear test using sandpaper simulated rough bark conditions; the scraper base length was measured before and after two sets of 20 scraping cycles.
- **Load bearing:** A qualitative dynamic load test applied maximum expected shear force to assess structural integrity of the press-fit joint and printed walls.

**Outcome:** The scraper successfully removed more than 90% of clay from 9 out of 10 simulated egg masses, with the single failure occurring on a cornered surface. The bowl captured 100% of detached debris, suggesting the width can be reduced by at least 1 inch in the next iteration. The press-fit joint and printed walls showed no visible yielding under load. However, the blade lost 0.4 cm of material over 40 sandpaper scrapes, confirming the plastic edge is insufficient for long-term use. The next prototype will transition to a harder material such as folded sheet metal or a metal insert. The flared walls will also be removed to allow scraping at wider angles, and a modular corner attachment will be explored.
